Research Interest

"Can you hear the shape of a drum?"

In 1911, Hermann Weyl proved the remarkable asymptotic formula describing distribution of (large) eigenvalues of the Dirichlet Laplacian in a bounded domain Ω ⊂ R d.

N(λ)=(2π)-d ω_d Vol(Ω) λ d/2 (1+ o(1)) as λ→ +∞, where N(λ) is the number of eigenvalues of the Laplacian spectrum, which are less than λ, ω_d is a volume of the unit ball in R_d, Vol(Ω) is the volume of Ω, and the Laplace spectrum of a domain Ω is defined as the set of positive real numbers λ (with multiplicities) that satisfy the eigenvalue problem in Ω with Dirichlet boundary conditions.

This result motivated the title of a famous paper by M. Kac
“Can you hear the shape of a drum?”.

The question is: can the shape of a bounded domain Ω ⊂ R d be determined by the Laplace spectrum?

Two domains are called isospectral if they have the same eigenvalues. Consider the space of domains with a smooth boundary. The existence of isospectral non-isometric domains is a well-known open question.

The first direction of my research is to prove the local spectral rigidity for convex planar domains, i.e. for a smooth convex planar domain Ω the Laplace spectrum determines Ω locally. There are no nearby isospectral non-isometric domains with smooth boundary. All of the these questions can also be posed for Riemannian manifolds. The questions of local spectral rigidity for Riemannian manifolds with Anosov geodesic flows are closely related and also open.

Another challenging question is Ivrii’s conjecture. There is a hope to disprove it and as the result show that error term in the Weyl law is not always the one you expect.

Locally integrable systems

This direction of research targets a proof of local rigidity for integrable systems: geodesic flows on tori (resp. convex planar billiards). The first goal is to prove that an integrable metric close to a Liouville metric is Liouville. The second type is billiards inside smooth planar domains integrable near the boundary.

Kirkwood gaps

Stochastic behavior in Kirkwood gaps

In the Solar system between orbits of Mars and Jupiter there are nearly two million asteroids of diameter over one km. When astronomers look distribution of asteroids with respect to semi-major axis or, equivalently, period, they see gaps in the distribution.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt#/media/File:Kirkwood_Gaps.svg
The gaps are located near low order resonances with Jupiter. The most famous gaps have period ratio with Jupiter 1:3, 2:5, 3:7. For the 1:3 gap there is a well-known mechanism proposed by Wisdom, which is supported by numerical experiments. This mechanism seems to apply also to the 2:5 gap. Our goal is two fold: one is to develop a mathematic theory of stochastic behavior at the gaps 1:3, 2:5, 3:7 and explain the shape of the distribution of these gaps.
